Functions: The Core Concept

So, first of all: what is a function? Take a second. What comes to your mind when you hear the word "function"?

You might be thinking of variables, or a formula, or an input and an output. And all of these are correct to different degrees! But I want to flesh this out a bit more.

Broadly speaking, a function is a transformation. It is a map.

You can think of it kind of like a box. You put something in, and you get something out. And exactly what you get out depends on what this function is, and what the input is.

A great way to think about this is like cooking. Think of your kitchen.

Suppose your input is a basket of raw ingredients: potatoes, cauliflower, onions, tomatoes, and some basic spices.

Now, I can apply one recipe to those ingredients: I chop everything, toss it in a pan, and dry-roast it with cumin and turmeric. My output is a dry Aloo Gobi sabzi.

But what if I apply a different recipe to those exact same ingredients? This time, I puree the onions and tomatoes, create a rich base, and simmer the vegetables in it. My output is now a thick Aloo Gobi masala curry.

The ingredients didn't change. The recipe changed. The recipe is the function. It is the specific set of steps that transforms your input into your output.

Typically, the way we've thought of functions algebraically is that we call the input X, and we call the output Y. So we write Y equals f of X.

For example, if our function is Y equals 2 plus X, and we put a 0 in, we get a 2 out. If we put a 1 in, we get a 3 out. The output varies precisely according to the rule we set for X.

The core idea I want you to hold onto right now is simply this: a function is a transformation process. It is the exact set of instructions that maps your input to your output.

Representing Functions

So, we know that a function is a transformation. It maps an input to an output. But how do we actually write that down? How do we describe a function to someone else?

There are multiple ways to describe a function, and I want to talk about three specific representations today. The most important thing to remember is that all three are equivalent.

The first way is the one we just looked at: the Formula, or the algebraic representation.

This is when we write out the math equation. For example, Y equals 2X plus 1. Or Y equals the sine of X. We are giving a specific, compact mathematical rule.

The beauty of a formula is that it is incredibly compact, and it works for any valid value of X. You just substitute your X into the equation, and out pops your Y.

But sometimes, a formula is hard to visualize. So we can use a second representation: a Table.

Imagine a simple table with two columns. The left column is X, the right column is Y. We take a few values of X—say, 0, 1, 2, and 3—plug them into our formula, and write the resulting Y in the next column.

A table is fantastic because it requires zero computation for the user. It is just a quick lookup. If X is 2, I look at the table, and I instantly see Y is 5.

The trade-off is that a table takes up a lot of space. And since there are infinite possible numbers, a table can never show you every single value. It only shows you a snapshot.

That brings us to the third representation, and this one is absolutely critical for building intuition: the Graph, or the visual representation.

A graph takes our table and draws it as a picture. We put our input X on the horizontal axis, and our output Y on the vertical axis. For every pair of X and Y from our table, we put a dot on the screen.

If we calculate enough dots, they merge together to form a continuous curve or a straight line.

A graph is incredibly powerful because human beings are visual creatures. One glance at a graph tells you exactly how the input and output relate. Is the output growing? Is it shrinking? Is it waving back and forth?

As we move forward, we will constantly transform functions from one form to another.

We might take an algebraic formula and plot it on a graph to understand its shape. Or, we might start with a massive table of data, plot it on a graph, and try to discover the formula that created it.

It doesn't matter if I give you a formula, a table, or a graph. You can always work between them. They are just three different languages telling the exact same story.

Meet the Function Families

So, we've seen that any mathematical formula can be translated into a picture on a graph.

Now, if we just started creating random formulas and graphing them, you might expect to see complete chaos—just random lines and squiggles everywhere.

But that is not what happens.

Instead, we start to notice patterns. We realize that functions aren't just a random assortment of rules. They naturally group themselves into distinct families.

Just like human families share DNA and physical traits, function families share algebraic structures and visual shapes. Functions in the same family behave in similar, predictable ways.

If you can look at a formula and immediately recognize which family it belongs to, you already know half the story of how it will behave before you even draw the graph.

In our work, we are going to rely heavily on three specific families. Let's look at their characteristics, starting with the most foundational one...

The Polynomial Family

The first, and arguably the most foundational family we are going to look at, is the Polynomial.

Let's look at the general formula. It looks something like this: f of X equals W sub 0, plus W sub 1 times X, plus W sub 2 times X squared, and so on.

You will often see this written using a Greek letter Sigma, which just stands for summation. It might look intimidating, but if you have ever written a program, this is quite literally just a for loop adding terms together.

Now, there is a critical distinction here. The X is your input data. But the W's—W0, W1, W2—are your Parameters, or your weights. Think of the W's as physical knobs or dials on our machine. By turning these knobs, we change the shape of our function.

The highest power of X in our equation is called the degree of the polynomial. We classify polynomials by this degree. Let's build them up, starting from zero.

If our degree is zero, we only have the first term. f of X equals W0. Let's say W0 is 5. We get f of X equals 5.

If we graph this, it is a perfectly horizontal straight line. No matter what X is, Y is always 5. The input has absolutely no effect on the output. In machine learning, this is our baseline.

Now, let's turn the dial and increase the degree to 1. We get f of X equals W0, plus W1 times X.

When you graph this, you still get a straight line, but now it has a tilt. It has a constant rate of change.

The parameter W1 is the slope. It controls how steep the line is, and whether it points up or down. The parameter W0 is the intercept. It just slides the whole line up and down, controlling exactly where it crosses the Y-axis.

This degree-one polynomial is the workhorse of basic predictive modeling. When we do Linear Regression, this is exactly the machine we are building.

Let's go one step further. A polynomial of degree 2. We get W0, plus W1 times X, plus W2 times X squared.

This is a quadratic function, and its graph is no longer straight. It is a parabola—a U-shape or an upside-down U. The rate of change is now accelerating.

More importantly, it has a single lowest point, or a single highest point. Keep this shape in the back of your mind. In machine learning, we are always trying to minimize our errors. Our error curves often look exactly like this U-shape, and finding the bottom of this bowl is fundamentally how neural networks learn.

We can keep adding higher degrees—X to the third, X to the fourth. As we do, our graph gets more flexible. It gains more bends, more peaks, and more valleys.

But there is a catch. If we use a very high-degree polynomial, it can snake its way through every single point of our training data perfectly. But it becomes wildly unpredictable everywhere else. This is a sneak peek at a huge concept in machine learning called overfitting.

Exponentials and Logarithms

Now let's move to our next function families: Exponentials and Logarithms. These two are intrinsically linked, and they are absolutely brilliant for dealing with values that span a massive number of magnitudes.

Let's start with the Exponential function. In a polynomial, like X squared, the X is on the ground. But in an exponential function, the variable X moves upstairs into the exponent.

The general formula is f of X equals b to the power of X. That "b" is called the base.

The behavior of this function completely changes depending on what that base is.

If the base is greater than 1, say 2 to the power of X, the graph shows explosive growth. It starts flat, hits the Y-axis at 1, and then shoots almost straight up.

If the base is exactly 1, well, 1 to any power is just 1. So you get a boring, horizontal straight line. Not very useful for us.

But if the base is between 0 and 1, like 0.5 to the power of X, you get exponential decay. The graph starts incredibly high, drops drastically, and then levels out hugging the zero line.

But here is the most crucial, universal rule about exponential functions: the output is always positive.

It does not matter if your input X is positive a million, or negative a million. If you look at the graph, it never, ever dips below the horizontal axis. It gets closer and closer to zero, but it never crosses it.

Because they handle massive scale so well, exponential relationships are incredibly common in the real world. Think about the Richter scale for earthquakes, where a 6.0 is ten times more powerful than a 5.0. Or Decibels for measuring sound volume. They are everywhere.

Which brings us to its partner: the Logarithmic family.

You can think of the logarithm as the exact inverse—the undo button—of the exponential function. If you take the exponential graph and mirror it diagonally, you get the log graph.

Visually, it climbs up, but it grows slower and slower as you move to the right. And as X gets close to zero, the graph plunges straight down into a bottomless pit. Because it is the inverse of the exponential, you cannot take the log of a negative number or zero.

But there is one specific mathematical property of logarithms that makes them the best friend of any computer scientist or machine learning engineer.

The rule is this: The log of A times B, equals the log of A, plus the log of B.

Logarithms magically transform multiplication into addition. Why do we care? Because of machine precision.

In machine learning, we are often multiplying thousands of probabilities together. Probabilities are numbers less than 1. If you multiply 0.1 by 0.1 by 0.1 a thousand times, the number becomes so microscopically small that a computer literally cannot hold it in memory. It just gives up and calls it zero. We call this underflow.

But, if we take the logarithm of those tiny numbers first, that multiplication turns into addition. The numbers stay manageable. We save the computer's memory, and we save our model.

Trigonometric Functions: The Math of Cycles

We have one last major family to cover today. We've seen polynomials that eventually shoot off to infinity, and exponentials that explode or decay. But what happens if our data doesn't do either of those things? What if it repeats?

Enter the Trigonometric functions, specifically sinusoids like the sine and cosine waves.

The most defining feature of a sine wave is that it is bounded. It is trapped between a ceiling and a floor, usually positive 1 and negative 1. No matter how massive your input X gets, the output Y will just keep smoothly undulating up and down within those limits.

These functions represent the mathematics of cycles, oscillation, and rhythm.

Just like our polynomial had knobs we could turn, a sine wave has parameters that change its shape. The best way to understand these knobs is to think about sound.

A perfectly "pure" audio tone is actually just a single sine wave passing through the air.

Our first knob is Frequency. This is how fast or slow the wave repeats itself. In audio, a change in frequency is a change in pitch. A wave squished tightly together is a high frequency, giving you a high-pitched note. A wave stretched out is a low frequency, giving you a deep bass note.

Our second knob is Amplitude. This controls how tall the peaks are, and how deep the valleys go. In our audio example, amplitude is simply volume. A taller wave is a louder sound.

Our third knob is Phase, which simply shifts the entire wave a little bit to the left or the right along the axis.

So, why do we care about waves in machine learning? Because the real world is driven by seasonality.

If you are building a time-series model to predict retail sales, or website traffic, or daily temperatures, your data will have daily, weekly, or yearly cycles. Sinusoids are exactly how we mathematically model that seasonality.

Furthermore, it turns out that any repeating pattern in the universe—no matter how jagged, weird, or complex—can be perfectly recreated just by adding a bunch of simple sine and cosine waves together.

And if you eventually study Large Language Models, you'll find that they use these exact sine waves just to keep track of the order of words in a sentence, purely because of those unique repeating frequencies.

So, we have our Polynomials for basic trends, our Exponentials and Logs for massive scale, and our Sinusoids for cycles. These are the tools in our toolbox.
