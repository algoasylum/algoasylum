Functions: The Building Blocks of Machine Learning

Okay, so before we dive deeper into our algorithms, I want to take a step back. We need to talk about some of the fundamental mathematics that form the basis for everything we do in machine learning.

This might be a bit repetitive for some of you, especially if you've done a lot of math in the past. But I think the way we are going to talk about it will give you a really good orientation. It's absolutely important that you understand this well, because literally everything we do in machine learning is built around functions of some sort or the other.

So, first of all: what is a function? Take a second. What comes to your mind when you hear the word "function"?

You might be thinking of variables, or a formula, or an input and an output. And all of these are correct to different degrees! But I want to flesh this out a bit more.

Broadly speaking, a function is a transformation. It is a map.

You can think of it kind of like a box. You put something in, and you get something out. And exactly what you get out depends on what this function is, and what the input is.

Typically, the way we've thought of functions algebraically is that we call the input X, and we call the output Y. So we write Y equals f of X.

Now, a common misconception—and this trips up a lot of people when they first come back to math—is thinking that one side is always a variable and the other is a constant. Let me show you why we have to be careful with that thinking.

Suppose I tell you my function is f of x equals 5.

If my input X is 0, what is my output? It's 5.
What if X is 1? It's still 5.
What if X is 10,000? The output is exactly 5.

Because there is no X on the right side of that equation, the box just does the same thing no matter what you put into it. So yes, the output is a constant 5, but it is still a function. It is a transformation that transforms everything into 5.

But in most cases, your output will vary according to X. If our function is Y equals 2 plus X, and we put a 0 in, we get a 2 out. If we put a 1 in, we get a 3 out.

And this brings us to a very important rule about this box. It is strictly predictable. For any given input you put in, the box must always give you the exact same output. If you put a 1 in today and get a 3, you have to get a 3 tomorrow.

Now, before we move on, I want to connect this directly to data science. It is the exact same concept, just with different labels.

In math, we talk about the input X. In machine learning, we call this our Features or our observations.

In math, we have the output Y. In machine learning, we call this our Target or our label.

And the function itself? That box? That is our Model.

And here is the crucial shift in mindset.

In traditional math, I give you the input X, and I give you the formula for the box, and I ask you to calculate the output Y.

But in data science and machine learning, the data comes to us as historical observations. We already have the inputs, and we already know what the outputs were. What we don't have is the formula.

Our entire goal in machine learning is to look at the inputs and the outputs, and figure out what the box is. We are trying to figure out that formula.
